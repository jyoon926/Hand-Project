Error Analysis

Relative Error = Absolute Error / Expected Value

Since we don’t know the actual areas of our hands, in order to prove that our area approximations have relative errors of less than 1%, we can use our highest resolution images as a benchmark for the errors of the lower resolution images and thus derive whether or not our highest resolution area approximation has a relative error of less than 1%.

As such, we’ve compared low-resolution (32x32) versions of each of our images to our most precise, high-resolution images (2048x2048). The results are below:

Heewon Ahn

Low-resolution area: 152.343 cm2

High-resolution area: 152.597 cm2

Relative error = (152.497-152.343) / 152.597 = 0.00101

0.101%

Jacob Yoon

Low-resolution area: 148.562 cm2

High-resolution area: 147.446 cm2

Relative error = (148.562 - 147.446) / 147.446 = 0.00732

0.732%

Benigno McKain

Low-resolution area: 163.582 cm2

High-resolution area: 163.404 cm2

Relative error = (163.582 - 163.404) / 163.404 = 0.001089

0.109%

Jack Frazier

Low-resolution area: 172.872 cm2

High-resolution area: 174.431 cm2

Relative error = (174.431-172.872) / 174.431 = 0.00894

0.894%

All of the relative errors of the lowest resolution images are less than 1%. To add, the relative errors decrease rapidly as the resolution increases. For instance, using the 128x128 images yields relative errors of less than 0.2% for each of the hands. Thus, we can confidently state that the 2048x2048 area approximations have relative errors of far less than 1% compared to the actual areas.
